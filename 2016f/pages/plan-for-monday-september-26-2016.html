  • 1981

  • 114 deaths, 200 injuries

  • Numerous people standing on suspended walkways

  • Alteration of original design

  • Findings:

    • The maximum load capacity of the fourth floor walkway was only 53% the maximum load capacity of Kansas City Building Code standards
    • The fabrication alterations from the original design doubled the load that was received by the fourth floor walkway
    • The deformation and distortion of the fourth floor hanger rods support the notion that the collapse began at that point
    • No evidence that the quality of construction or material selection played a role in the walkway collapse.

Strength 9:16¶

  • Max stress of any part must be < strength
  • strength: property of the component/part/element
  • \(S\) typically denotes strength
  • stress: a property of the component's state at a specific point
  • \(\sigma\) normal stress and \(\tau\) shear stress
  • Needs margin to ensure failure rate or improbable
  • Focus on areas of the part that are critical stress areas
Strength
Inherent property of the object geometry and material. S, Sy, Su
Stress
Property of the object's state and location. sigma, tau

Design Factor and Factor of safety 9:22¶

Design Factor
The pre-design target safety margin, i.e. the desired factor of strength over max allowable strength. "All elevators should have a design factor of 10."
Factor of Safety
The post design, i.e. actual, minimum factor of strength. "The factor of safety of this elevator is 11.3."

FoS should always be > than the DF!

FoS is deterministic based on absolutes

Any ideas why the DF and FoS are different?
Mention Table A-17 of preferred sizes.

Factor of Safety Method

  • Analyze all loss of function modes
  • Choose mode that leads to smallest design factor to govern decisions
\begin{equation*} n_d = \frac{loss of function parameter}{maximum allowable parameter} \end{equation*}

Class Question¶

If the load that will cause failure is between 90 and 110 lbs and you'd like a design factor of 2, what is the max allowable load?

\begin{equation*} P_{max} = \frac{P_{fail}}{n_d} = \frac{90 \textrm{ lbs}}{2} = 45 \textrm{ lbs} \end{equation*}

This is most typically:

\begin{equation*} n_d = \frac{S}{\sigma_{max} \textrm{ or } \tau_{max}} \end{equation*}

because stress may not vary linearly with load.

Uncertainty

  • Gaussian distributions can model many real world observations
  • We can make predictions of quality, strenght, loads, etc in a stochastic manner.
  • Probabilities are the area under the Gaussian probaility density curve and are found by integration.
  • The book provides a table of probabilities for a nominal Gaussian curve.

Selection of Design Factor

  • subjective
  • follow industry standards and codes

Depends on:

  • degree of uncertainty about loading
  • degreee of uncertainty about material strength and structure
  • consequences of failure > human safety, economics
  • cost of providing a high factor of safety

Boils down to the probability of failure.
